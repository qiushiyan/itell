---
title: Key Terms
---

<Keyterm label="aggregate demand (AD)">
  the amount of total spending on domestic goods and services in an economy
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="aggregate demand (AD) curve">
  the total spending on domestic goods and services at each price level
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="aggregate demand/aggregate supply model">
  a model that shows what determines total supply or total demand for the
  economy, and how total demand and total supply interact at the macroeconomic
  level
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="aggregate supply (AS)">
  the total quantity of output (i.e. real GDP) firms will produce and sell
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="aggregate supply (AS) curve">
  the total quantity of output (i.e. real GDP) that firms will produce and sell
  at each price level
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="full-employment GDP">
  another name for potential GDP, when the economy is producing at its potential
  and unemployment is at the natural rate of unemployment
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="intermediate zone">
  portion of the SRAS curve where GDP is below potential but not so far below as
  in the Keynesian zone; the SRAS curve is upward-sloping, but not vertical in
  the intermediate zone
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="Keynes' law">“demand creates its own supply”</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="Keynesian zone">
  portion of the SRAS curve where GDP is far below potential and the SRAS curve
  is flat
</Keyterm>
long run aggregate supply (LRAS) curve vertical line at potential GDP showing no
relationship between the price level for output and real GDP in the long run

<Keyterm label="neoclassical economists">
  economists who generally emphasize the importance of aggregate supply in
  determining the size of the macroeconomy over the long run
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="neoclassical zone">
  portion of the SRAS curve where GDP is at or near potential output where the
  SRAS curve is steep
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="potential GDP">
  the maximum quantity that an economy can produce given full employment of its
  existing levels of labor, physical capital, technology, and institutions
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="Say's law">“supply creates its own demand”</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="short run aggregate supply (SRAS) curve">
  positive short run relationship between the price level for output and real
  GDP, holding the prices of inputs fixed
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="stagflation">
  an economy experiences stagnant growth and high inflation at the same time
</Keyterm>
