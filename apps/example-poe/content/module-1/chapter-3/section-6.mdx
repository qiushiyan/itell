---
title: Key Terms
---

<Keyterm label="ceteris paribus">other things being equal</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="complements">
  goods that are often used together so that consumption of one good tends to
  enhance consumption of the other
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="consumer surplus ">
  the extra benefit consumers receive from buying a good or service, measured by
  what the individuals would have been willing to pay minus the amount that they
  actually paid
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="deadweight loss">
  the loss in social surplus that occurs when a market produces an inefficient
  quantity
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="demand">
the relationship between price and the quantity demanded of a certain good or service

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="demand curve">
  a graphic representation of the relationship between price and quantity
  demanded of a certain good or service, with quantity on the horizontal axis
  and the price on the vertical axis
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="demand schedule">
  a table that shows a range of prices for a certain good or service and the
  quantity demanded at each price
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="economic surplus ">see social surplus</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="equilibrium">
  the situation where quantity demanded is equal to the quantity supplied; the
  combination of price and quantity where there is no economic pressure from
  surpluses or shortages that would cause price or quantity to change
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="equilibrium price">
  the price where quantity demanded is equal to quantity supplied
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="equilibrium quantity">
  the quantity at which quantity demanded and quantity supplied are equal for a
  certain price level
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="excess demand">
  at the existing price, the quantity demanded exceeds the quantity supplied;
  also called a shortage
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="excess supply">
  at the existing price, quantity supplied exceeds the quantity demanded; also
  called a surplus
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="factors of production">
  the resources such as labor, materials, and machinery that are used to produce
  goods and services; also called inputs
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="inferior good">
  a good in which the quantity demanded falls as income rises, and in which
  quantity demanded rises and income falls
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="inputs">
  the resources such as labor, materials, and machinery that are used to produce
  goods and services; also called factors of production
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="law of demand">
  the common relationship that a higher price leads to a lower quantity demanded
  of a certain good or service and a lower price leads to a higher quantity
  demanded, while all other variables are held constant
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="law of supply">
  the common relationship that a higher price leads to a greater quantity
  supplied and a lower price leads to a lower quantity supplied, while all other
  variables are held constant
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="normal good">
  a good in which the quantity demanded rises as income rises, and in which
  quantity demanded falls as income falls
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="price">
  what a buyer pays for a unit of the specific good or service
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="price ceiling">a legal maximum price</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="price control">
  government laws to regulate prices instead of letting market forces determine
  prices
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="price floor">a legal minimum price</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="producer surplus">
  the extra benefit producers receive from selling a good or service, measured
  by the price the producer actually received minus the price the producer would
  have been willing to accept
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="quantity demanded">
the total number of units of a good or service consumers are willing to purchase at a given price

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="quantity supplied">

the total number of units of a good or service producers are willing to sell at a given price

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="shift in demand">

when a change in some economic factor (other than price) causes a different quantity to be demanded at every price

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="shift in supply">

when a change in some economic factor (other than price) causes a different quantity to be supplied at every price

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="shortage">

at the existing price, the quantity demanded exceeds the quantity supplied; also called excess demand

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="social surplus">

the sum of consumer surplus and producer surplus

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="substitute">
  a good that can replace another to some extent, so that greater consumption of
  one good can mean less of the other
</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="supply">
the relationship between price and the quantity supplied of a certain good or service

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="supply curve">

a line that shows the relationship between price and quantity supplied on a graph, with quantity supplied on the horizontal axis and price on the vertical axis

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="supply schedule">

a table that shows a range of prices for a good or service and the quantity supplied at each price

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="surplus">

at the existing price, quantity supplied exceeds the quantity demanded; also called excess supply

</Keyterm>

<Keyterm label="total surplus">

see social surplus

</Keyterm>
